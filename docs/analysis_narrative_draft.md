# Overview

This project aims to examine how macroeconomic conditions influence sector-level equity performance using Exchange Traded Funds (ETF). The goal is to gain understanding of how these varying environments shape both profit and risk across areas of the sock market. A set of eleven U.S. equity sectors were chosen for this analysis, including Materials (XLB), Communication Services (XLC), Financials (XLF), Energy (XLE), Industrials (XLI), Technology (XLK), Consumer Staples (XLP), Real Estate (XLRE), Utilities (XLU), Health Care (XLV) and Consumer Discretionary (XLY). To capture changing economic situations seen in real-world stock data, the data being used was categorized into multiple macroeconomic regimes:

-   **Combined Macro-Regimes**, which simultaneously classify environments based on inflation (low/high) and interest rates (rising/falling).
-   **Inflation Regimes**, which classify envrionments by seperating low vs. high inflation periods
-   **Interest Rate Regimes** , distinguish falling vs. rising interest rate environments
-   **VIX Regimes**, represents market volatility, classifying it as either a calm or stress environment.

By comparing sector ETF returns and volatility across identifiable regimes, analysis can determine which sectors are most sensitive to regime changes and how market risk shifts under various conditions. The final dataset spans sector-level data from 2000 to 2026 capturing multiple economic cycles, such as periods or recession and market stress. Additionally, the dataset contains over 66,000 observations, ensuring consistency and efficient sampling in the merged time-series structure. However, there are missing values across several variables, particularly in sector returns, suggesting difficulties in data accessibility overtime.

# Key Findings

## Initial Insights

Primarily viewing both daily and annualized returns by regime demonstrates that volatility regime strongly matters in the context of sector regime. In both tables ETF returns are consistently negative across sectors under the VIX-stress condition. Another pattern that initially stood out was the consistency in positive returns across sectors in both falling and rising interest rate conditions. Even in falling interest rate conditions sector ETF returns fail to drop below 0.20% suggesting that rate regime split is too coarse to create a strong distinction in sector returns. The inflation regime depicts the same weak separation between split conditions and having marginal impact on sector returns. Since volatility shows the clearest split in sector returns, it was chosen for initial sector interpretation. Under stress technology seems to have the weakest returns, as indicated by its low percent average return of -0.23%, this was confirmed in the annualized returns table, under the same VIX-stress condition technology yields a -43.45% percent annual average. Consumer staples, however, seems more defensive to changing conditions, though its still negative under stress, it has a smaller decline than the other sectors. This defense was confirmed in the Best & Worst Sector returns table, which showed Consumer staples as the best sector under VIX-stress conditions. Furthermore, the stress regime also gave insight to cyclical sectors like Technology (–0.23%), Financials (–0.22%), and Communication Services (–0.22%). These sectors do well in economic expansion and low-stress regimes, but do poorly in recession and high inflation periods. This reveals that under extreme conditions investors usually pull out of technology and finance, to protect their profit from losses.

## Combined Macro-Regimes

The combined macro environment drives a consistent premium in sector returns, with the "High Inflation / Falling Rates" regime acting as a clear performance "sweet spot," where almost every sector reaches its peak average return. In contrast, regimes with low inflation show significantly small returns, therefore in future analysis a baseline of inflationary activity may have to be imposed to support stronger sector-level growth in this sample. Materials (XLB) and Energy (XLE), show consistent high performance across all regimes. Energy, specifically ranks in the top 3 under ever condition, signifying that along with Materials, they have a structural play in sector ETF returns regardless of environment conditions. Financials exhibit reliability across both high and low inflation/ rate environments. It remains in the middle-top tier across all conditions, displaying that Financials are relatively robust across macro regimes and do not exhibit strong sensitivity to inflation or interest rate environments. Technology (XLK) is the primary regime-dependent sector, ranking number 1 with a percent daily return range (0.8%-0.11%) under the High Inflation/rate conditions but drops to a range (0.00%- 0.01%) in Low Inflation/rate conditions. This suggests that technology's sector growth is highly sensitive to inflation conditions in comparison to others. As justified by the single-factor tables inflation supports the "sweet spot" with High Inflation regimes capturing the highest return averages than low-inflation regimes.

## Interest Rates

While rising vs. falling rates don’t trigger a binary shift from negative to positive returns, they do cause significant rank reordering (e.g., for Technology and Communication Services), proving that rate regimes act as a tactical signal for sector rotation rather than an absolute indicator of market direction. Communication Services (XLC) shows high sensitivity to rates. It fluctuates between being the 1 and 2 performer under rising rates and falling to rank 9 in the falling rates regime. Inevitably showing, that the sector is particularly sensitive to inflationary pressures and cost-of-capital dynamics, as evidenced by its capital-intensive and rate-sensitive nature. This concludes that sectors do not react randomly. Instead, they exhibit consistent betas per macro regime. For example, Energy and Materials are structural inflation-hedging sectors, while Financials and Communication Services rotate based on the interest-rate cycle.

# Volatility Insights

In the VIX Regime – Avg Daily Return (%) table, the contrast between calm and stress regimes provides a clear framework for how volatility governs market returns. In the stable "calm" regime, the data shows uniformly positive returns across all sectors (ranging from +0.07% to +0.14%). This indicates a market environment where uncertainty is low, risk appetite is high, and the overall direction of the market lifts all sectors. In the "stress" regime, the market undergoes a fundamental breakdown in performance. The returns for all sectors flip to negative (ranging from -0.10% to -0.23%), signaling that systemic uncertainty forces a broad-based exit from equity risk. Vulnerable cyclical sectors, Technology (-0.23%), Communication Services (-0.22%), and Financials (-0.22%) show the largest declines, indicating they are most heavily penalized when market volatility spikes. The clear distinction in directional shift under split conditions evidences that market volatility is the greatest determinant of directional return in this sample. While other regimes (inflation/rates) may shift rankings (which sector is #1 vs #2), the VIX regime dictates the direction (positive vs. negative) of the entire portfolio. Additionally, the S&P 500 Historical Drawdown (Underwater Plot) shows that deep, prolonged drawdowns (e.g., 2008 GFC at \~55% or the 2000 Dot-com bust at \~49%) are the historical engines that drive how sectors perform in the "Stress" regime. The plot also shows that while some crashes are deep (COVID-19 at \~34%), the market’s recovery speed varies based on whether the stress is structural or a shock event. All in all validates the volatility patterns seen and why combined macro regimes are so critical—they capture the multi-dimensional pressure that shows up in the drawdown plot as a decline of a quarter of the market's value.

# Interpretation

Interpreting these patterns in an economic context highlights how sector performance is shaped by underlying market dynamics rather than being uniform across conditions. Inflation often accompanies periods of economic expansion and healthy rate growth. In conditions when the economy is growing and the demand is strong companies can expand their profit margins without losing a consumer base, which inevitably protects profit and maintains stock levels. Low inflation periods, however, reflect weaker economic conditions where companies have less opportunity to raise prices, and expectations of future growth are limited, which leads to the weak returns seen in the results. To interpret interest regime results, rising interest rates increase the discount rate used to value future cash flows, which lowers present values. Though this only occurs when the economy is strong enough to handle tight monetary policy. Certain markets also do well at anticipate these changes and hyper-fixate on the quickness of the changes rather than the increase in rate. When returns stay positive during higher-rate periods, it suggests that strong growth expectations are balancing out the negative effect of more expensive borrowing. VIX which measures the expected cost of portfolio insurance, demonstrated that systemic stress has the power to trump all other macroeconomic conditions. When market volatility rises, uncertainty is high and investors tend to sell stocks across the board regardless of the strength of the sector, regardless of fundamentals. Since different assets start moving together, the usual differences in sector performance largely disappear due to widespread selling.

# Limitations

## Regime-based Classification

The regime classification was smoothed out between condition splits due to our binary classification (rising/falling rates), consequently ignoring the magnitude and velocity of the changes. For example, a 50 point hike is treated the same as a 5 point increase and assigned a "Rising" state. This could describe why we see all positive values in the interest rate regime and no nuances between sectors.

## Sample Period/ Time-Sampling

The data covers a wide sample ranging over various economic conditions (2000-2026), which encapsulate markets that have gone under extremely stressful conditions, throughout history. These historical market tragedies, such as the 2020 COVID-19 market crash, make findings unable to be generalized to normal market fluctuations, especially when extreme months account for the disproportion seen in the "stress" regime. Furthermore, a critical limitation of the time-sampling was the uneven sample size per regime. Since certain regimes (e.g., "Low Inflation / Falling Rates") represented a significantly smaller portion of the total historical sample than others, the mean returns for those regimes were less statistically reliable and more susceptible to extreme outliers. For example looking at Energy (XLE) in "avg_return_summary.csv", in the low inflation regime Energy has 3581 observations, but for the falling interest rate regime there were 2870 observations.

## Over-Interpretation of Mean Returns

Relying on simple mean returns provides a directional overview but lacks statistical control. Without controlling for other confounding factors, such as: sector-specific risk, changes in corporate earnings, or company-specific exposures, it is risky to attribute performance strictly to the macro regime changes. The sector means could be heavily influenced by isolated events rather than structural macro-sensitivity.

## Instrument-Specific Caveats

Using Sector ETFs (e.g., XLK, XLE) introduces limitations inherent to index construction. ETF's are regularly updated, so the companies that are seen in the data presently are not the same as being seen during the 2008 crisis. Also Sector ETF weighs by company size which causes larger companies to outperform others in certain sectors. Leading to the sector-homogeneity assumption, which assumes that all companies within a sector act the same during macro economic changes. Since sectors are composed of diverse companies with varying business models, the results may mask significant sub-sector data and risks that are not captured by looking at macro regimes.

# Conclusion

Overall, sector performance varies meaningfully across market regimes rather than remaining consistent over time. The findings confirm that while individual sectors possess distinct sensitivities, their performance is ultimately governed by the macro regime in which they operate. Cyclical sectors tend to outperform during periods of strong growth and stable conditions, while more defensive sectors perform relatively better during weaker or uncertain environments. In contrast, high-volatility or stress regimes are characterized by broad-based declines and increased correlation across sectors, limiting diversification benefits. Volatility remains low and returns more stable in calm market conditions but rises sharply during periods of uncertainty, driving widespread risk-off behavior. While these results should be interpreted with some caution due to data limitations, they clearly demonstrate broader implications that macroeconomic conditions play a key role in shaping both sector returns and market volatility. Integrating this macroeconomic context remains a critical component of informed investment analysis, providing a clearer framework for navigating different phases of the economic cycle.
